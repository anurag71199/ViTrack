High Blood Pressure (Hypertension)

Causes of High Blood Pressure

  • Age - High blood pressure is more common in men at the age of 60 and above. Women are more likely to develop high blood pressure after age 65.
  • Family history - If someone in the family has a history of high blood pressure, then it is more likely that the future generation may also have it
  • Being overweight - If people don’t manage their diet and eat unhealthy food, they tend to have high blood pressure
  • Not being physically active - People who don’t exercise or sit ideally, tend to have higher heart rates
  • Using tobacco - Smoking or chewing tobacco raises your blood pressure temporarily
  • Too much salt (sodium) in your diet - Too much sodium in your diet can cause an increase in your blood pressure.
  • Drinking too much alcohol - If women have more than one drink a day and men have more than two drinks a day, it may affect their blood pressure
  • Stress - Stress can lead to an increase in blood pressure

What can happen due to high blood pressure?

  • Heart attack - High blood pressure might lead to a heart attack or a stroke
  • Aneurysm - High blood pressure might cause your blood vessels to weaken and bulge, forming an aneurysm
  • Heart failure - High blood pressure might cause a workload on your heart leading to heart failure.
  • Thickened or torn blood vessels in the eyes - High blood pressure might tear the blood vessels in your eyes resulting in vision loss.
  • Metabolic syndrome - High blood pressure may lead to metabolic syndrome which includes disorders related to your body like high waist size, decrease in good cholesterol, increase in insulin levels etc.
  • Trouble with memory or understanding - Increased blood pressure might affect your ability to remember and learn.

How can it be prevented? OR How to lower the blood pressure?

  • Exercise more. Don’t be inactive
  • If you’re overweight, lose weight and maintain your diet.
  • Eat less sugar and salt(sodium)
  • Eat more potassium containing food
  • Stop smoking or chewing tobacco
  • Drink less alcohol
  • Reduce excess stress. Don’t overthink and keep your mind free
  • Make sure to get at least 7-8 hours of sleep
  • Try meditation or yoga

High Body Temperature (Fever/ Hyperthermia)

Symptoms

  • Shivering
  • Sweating
  • Headache
  • Body-ache
  • Irritable nature
  • Loss of appetite
  • General weakness

Causes of Hyperthermia

  • The most common cause of a fever is a virus or a bacterial infection.
  • Stomach bugs (gastroenteritis) can also cause elevated body temperature.
  • Other infections, such as infections of the ear, lungs, skin, throat, bladder or kidney, can also cause fever.
  • Sunburn or heat exhaustion can lead to a high body temperature.
  • One of the most tell-tale symptoms of COVID-19 is fever.
  • Certain inflammatory conditions such as rheumatoid arthritis can cause fever as well.
  • Some medications, such as antibiotics and drugs used to treat high blood pressure or seizures, can also cause fever.
  • Consumption of illegal drugs such as cocaine and methamphetamine can cause fever as well.

How to treat Hyperthermia at home?

  • Staying hydrated by drinking lots of clear liquids such as water, juices etc
  • Getting ample rest and sleep
  • Taking over-the-counter medications like ibuprofen and acetaminophen to reduce fever. Note the proper dosage, and don’t use them alongside other fever-reducing medications.
  • Keeping yourself cool with lightweight clothing and by removing extra layers of bedding. Cold baths, or ice cube baths should be avoided.

Prevention

Fever can be prevented by reducing exposure to infections and infectious diseases. This can be done by keeping the following tips in mind:

  • Washing hands regularly, especially after spending time in a crowded or public area
  • Trying to avoid touching nose and mouth with unwashed hands, as these are the main ways in which a virus or bacteria enters the body
  • Avoiding sharing utensils, such as glasses or water bottles, with others
